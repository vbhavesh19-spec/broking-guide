Evolution
1875
BSE founded as "The Native Share and Stock Brokers Association" in Dalal Street, Mumbai. Asia's oldest stock exchange. Trading was done under a banyan tree before a formal premises was established.
1988 / 1992
SEBI established in 1988 as a non-statutory body, granted statutory powers via the SEBI Act 1992. This gave SEBI authority to register, regulate and investigate all market intermediaries.
1992 / 1994
NSE incorporated in 1992, commenced equity trading in November 1994. NSE introduced screen-based electronic trading, replacing the open outcry system. This democratized access beyond Mumbai's broker circle.
1996
Demat system launched. NSDL (National Securities Depository Limited) became India's first depository, ending physical share certificates. CDSL followed in 1999. This eliminated forgery, theft, and bad delivery risks.
2000
Derivatives launched on NSE. Index Futures began on June 12, 2000. Index Options in June 2001, Stock Options and Futures in 2001-02. This transformed India into one of the world's largest derivatives markets by volume.
2010
Zerodha founded. India's first significant discount broker, charging flat Rs.20 per trade. This disrupted the full-service model, forcing the industry to reconsider its pricing and technology stack entirely.
2016
Sub-broker model abolished by SEBI. SEBI circular CIR/HO/MIRSD/MIRSD2/CIR/P/2016/110 replaced sub-brokers with Authorized Persons (APs), a pure agency model with stricter controls and no client fund handling.
2020-21
Peak Margin framework introduced. SEBI mandated brokers to collect full upfront margin from clients before order placement. This fundamentally changed leverage available to retail traders and the risk profile of the broking business.
2023
T+1 settlement fully implemented across all securities from January 27, 2023. India became one of the first major markets globally to move to T+1, reducing counterparty risk and unlocking capital faster for investors.
Types of Broking

Full-Service Brokers

Offer research, advisory, PMS (Portfolio Management Services), trading, and relationship managers. Revenue from percentage-based brokerage (0.2% to 0.5% on delivery). Examples: Motilal Oswal, ICICI Direct, HDFC Securities, Kotak Securities.

Discount Brokers

Technology-first, flat-fee pricing (typically Rs.20 per executed order or zero for delivery equity). No advisory, minimal human touchpoints. Examples: Zerodha, Upstox, Angel One, Groww, 5Paisa. Captured majority of new account openings since 2020.

Institutional Brokers

Serve FPIs (Foreign Portfolio Investors), domestic mutual funds, insurance companies, and proprietary trading firms. Provide DMA (Direct Market Access), block deal facilitation, and research. Revenue model is negotiated per institutional client.

Commodity Brokers

Members of MCX (Multi Commodity Exchange) and NCDEX (National Commodity and Derivatives Exchange). Facilitate trading in gold, silver, crude oil, agri-commodities. Regulated by SEBI (commodity derivatives came under SEBI in 2015 after FMC merger).

SEBI (Regulator)

Securities and Exchange Board of India. Statutory body under SEBI Act 1992. Regulates all market intermediaries: brokers, depositories, exchanges, investment advisers, research analysts, portfolio managers. Issues circulars that are binding on all participants.

Quasi-Judicial Can investigate, adjudicate, and impose penalties. Maximum penalty under SEBI Act: Rs.25 crore or three times profit.

🔄

NSE and BSE (Exchanges)

NSE (National Stock Exchange): Founded 1992, commenced operations 1994. India's largest exchange by turnover. Benchmark indices: Nifty 50, Bank Nifty, Nifty IT. Home of the largest F&O market by volume globally.

BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange): Asia's oldest exchange, 1875. Benchmark: Sensex (30 stocks). Larger number of listed companies (5,000+). Key for SME listings.

🔐

Clearing Corporations

NSCCL (NSE Clearing Limited): Clearing and settlement for NSE. Becomes central counterparty (CCP) to every trade. Novation: buyer to every seller, seller to every buyer. Eliminates bilateral counterparty risk.

ICCL (Indian Clearing Corporation Ltd): Clearing for BSE. Same CCP function. Both are SEBI-recognized Qualified Central Counterparties (QCCPs).

📂

Depositories (NSDL / CDSL)

NSDL (National Securities Depository Limited, est. 1996): Holds securities in electronic form. Promoted by NSE, UTI, IDBI. Typically used by institutional and HNI clients.

CDSL (Central Depository Services Ltd, est. 1999): Promoted by BSE. Larger retail base. Listed on NSE. DPs (Depository Participants) are the interface between depositories and investors.

🏢

Trading Members (Brokers)

SEBI-registered entities with exchange membership. Can trade on behalf of clients and proprietarily. Must maintain Net Worth requirements: Rs.1 crore for NSE CM segment, higher for additional segments. Subject to exchange inspection and SEBI audit.

Responsibilities include: order execution, contract note issuance, margin collection, client fund segregation, KYC, and regulatory reporting.

🤝

Authorized Persons (APs)

Agents of Trading Members. Cannot hold client funds or securities. Registered through the broker with the exchange. Earn revenue sharing from broker (typically 40 to 70% of brokerage generated from their clients). Full detail covered in Section 05.

🌐

FPIs and DIIs

FPIs (Foreign Portfolio Investors): Registered with SEBI. Include sovereign funds, pension funds, hedge funds, and foreign mutual funds. Major price setters in large-cap stocks. Tracked daily by NSDL FPI data.

DIIs (Domestic Institutional Investors): Indian mutual funds, insurance companies (LIC), and pension funds. Act as counter-balance to FPI flows. Combined AUM of MF industry exceeded Rs.60 lakh crore in 2024.

📊

Other Key Regulators

RBI: Regulates currency derivatives, government securities, and overseas investment. Monetary policy decisions are the single biggest macro driver for equity markets.

AMFI: Association of Mutual Funds in India. Regulates distribution. ARN (AMFI Registration Number) is mandatory for MF distributors. Not a statutory body but sets industry norms.

Trade Lifecycle

Understanding the full path from a client clicking "BUY" to securities landing in their demat account is essential. Each step has rules, risks, and responsibilities.

Step-by-Step Order Flow

Step 1: Order Placement

Client places an order via broker's app, website, or Call & Trade. The order enters the broker's OMS (Order Management System). RMS (Risk Management System) checks available margin. If insufficient margin, order is rejected before hitting the exchange.

⚠

SEBI Margin Rule

Since Sep 2021, brokers must collect 100% upfront margin (SPAN + Exposure for F&O, VAR + ELM for equity) before allowing an order. No leverage beyond collected margin is permissible.

Step 2: Exchange Matching

Order is routed to the exchange via the broker's EMS (Execution Management System) using FIX protocol or NSE/BSE NEAT/BOLT APIs. The exchange matching engine matches buy and sell orders on price-time priority. Trade confirmed back to broker in milliseconds.

NSE's matching engine processes millions of orders per second. Co-location facilities at NSE's data center in Mumbai allow HFT firms to achieve sub-millisecond response times.

Step 3: Trade Confirmation

Broker receives trade confirmation from exchange. Contract note is issued to client electronically within 24 hours of trade (SEBI requirement). The contract note details: trade date, ISIN, quantity, price, brokerage, STT, GST, and net obligation.

Contract notes are legally binding documents and must be digitally signed by the broker. Clients must raise disputes within the time specified on the contract note.

Step 4: Clearing (Novation)

NSCCL (for NSE trades) or ICCL (for BSE trades) steps in as Central Counterparty. Through legal novation, the original bilateral trade is replaced: NSCCL becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. This eliminates counterparty default risk for market participants.

Step 5: Settlement (T+1)

Pay-in Deadline: Selling clients must deliver shares (debit from demat) or funds to the clearing corporation by a specified time on T+1 (next business day). Shares move via NSDL/CDSL settlement mechanism.

Pay-out: Exchange pays funds to selling brokers and credits shares to buying brokers' pool accounts. Funds and shares then flow to respective client accounts.

Step 6: Short Delivery and Auction

If a seller fails to deliver shares on T+1 (short delivery), the exchange conducts a Close-Out/Auction. The exchange buys shares in the auction market and charges the cost plus a 20% penalty to the defaulting broker. This risk is passed to the client.

Delivery vs Intraday vs BTST

Delivery (CNC)

Buy and hold. Shares credited to demat after T+1 settlement. Full payment required. STT: 0.1% on both buy and sell.

Intraday (MIS)

Buy and sell on the same day. Auto square-off by broker if not closed manually. Lower margin required. STT: 0.025% on sell side only.

BTST / ATST

Buy Today Sell Tomorrow. Sell before shares are in your demat. Risk: if original seller defaults, you face short delivery penalty in subsequent auction. Not risk-free.

Equity Derivatives Deep Dive

Futures

A standardized contract to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date (expiry). Traded on margin (initial SPAN margin typically 10 to 20% of contract value).

Example: Nifty 50 Futures, Lot Size: 75 units. If Nifty is at 22,000, one lot = Rs.16,50,000 notional. SPAN margin approximately Rs.1,20,000 to Rs.1,50,000. Profit/loss is the full Rs.750 per 10-point move (75 units x Rs.10).

Expiry: Last Thursday of the month for NSE index and stock futures. If Thursday is a holiday, previous trading day.

Options

The right (not obligation) to buy (Call) or sell (Put) the underlying at the Strike Price on or before Expiry. Buyer pays Premium, has limited loss (premium paid). Seller receives premium, has theoretically unlimited loss on calls.

Example: Nifty 22,000 Call at Rs.100 premium, Lot Size 75. Cost to buy: Rs.7,500. Profit if Nifty expires at 22,200: (22,200 - 22,000 - 100) x 75 = Rs.7,500.

Weekly Expiry: Nifty 50 (Thursday), BankNifty (Wednesday), FinNifty (Tuesday). This drives enormous intraday volumes in 0DTE (zero days to expiry) options.

India VIX

India VIX (Volatility Index) measures expected 30-day volatility of Nifty 50, derived from Nifty option prices. A VIX above 20 indicates high fear. Below 15 indicates complacency. During COVID (March 2020), India VIX hit 83. Typically ranges 12-18 in normal markets.

The AP Model

The Authorized Person (AP) is the frontline of client acquisition and servicing in the broking business. Understanding this model is essential if you are entering the distribution side of the industry.

📋

SEBI Circular: Sub-broker to AP (2016)

SEBI circular CIR/HO/MIRSD/MIRSD2/CIR/P/2016/110 dated October 2016 abolished the sub-broker category and introduced Authorized Persons. All existing sub-brokers were required to migrate to the AP framework. Key change: APs do not have SEBI registration and cannot hold client funds.

What an AP Can and Cannot Do

AP Can

  • Source and onboard new clients for the broker
  • Assist clients in completing KYC documentation
  • Accept orders from clients and relay to broker/exchange
  • Provide information about products offered by the broker
  • Operate a branch office in the broker's name
  • Use broker's trading platform and back-office systems
  • Earn revenue sharing on brokerage generated from own clients

AP Cannot

  • Collect or handle client funds (cash, cheques, online transfers)
  • Hold client securities in any account
  • Issue contract notes or trade confirmations independently
  • Advertise as a separate entity or create own brand for broking
  • Trade on behalf of clients without explicit authorization
  • Appoint sub-APs or further delegate client relationships
  • Offer guaranteed returns or assured profits
AP Revenue Economics

How an AP Earns

The AP earns a percentage of the brokerage generated from clients they have onboarded. The split is negotiated with the broker and varies significantly:

💡

Worked Example

An AP's clients generate Rs.5 lakh/month in brokerage. At a 60% revenue share, the AP earns Rs.3 lakh/month gross. From this, the AP must pay office rent, salaries, and internet costs. Pure play F&O clients generate high brokerage volume but churn fast. Delivery clients are sticky but low brokerage.

AP Registration Process

Eligibility Requirements

  • Individual: minimum 10+2 qualification. SEBI exam: NISM Series I or equivalent for relevant segment
  • Firm/Company: Authorized representative must meet above qualifications
  • No prior regulatory debarment by SEBI, exchanges, or other financial regulators
  • Clean CIBIL record is typically required by broker (varies)

Registration Steps

  1. Identify sponsoring broker and sign AP agreement
  2. Submit application to exchange (NSE/BSE) through broker
  3. Exchange does background check and approves registration
  4. AP gets exchange registration number (visible on broker's website)
  5. GST registration required if annual revenue exceeds Rs.20 lakh threshold
  6. Annual renewal of registration with exchange
⚠

AP Liability Trap

While APs cannot hold client funds, they are contractually responsible to the broker for clients they introduced. If an AP onboards a client who later defaults on margin, the AP may face claims from the broker. Always ensure complete KYC and financial suitability assessment of clients you onboard. Document everything.

Margins & Risk

Margins are the financial guarantee that keeps the clearing system solvent. Understanding the margin framework is non-negotiable on the trading desk. Get this wrong and clients get auto-squared off, disputes follow, and penalties land on the broker.

Margin Components for F&O

SPAN Margin

Standard Portfolio Analysis of Risk. Developed by Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME). Used by NSE and BSE for futures and options.

Calculates worst-case one-day loss by simulating 16 risk scenarios: the underlying moves up or down in varying degrees, while volatility rises or falls. The highest loss across all 16 scenarios is the SPAN margin requirement.

SPAN is recalculated intraday. A sharp move in the market mid-session can trigger a margin call even on open positions.

Exposure Margin

An additional buffer over SPAN, charged by exchanges. Covers residual risk not captured by SPAN scenarios. Typically:

  • Index Futures: 3% of contract value
  • Stock Futures: 5% of contract value (higher for illiquid stocks)
  • Options (sellers only): 3% for index, 5% for stocks

Total F&O Margin = SPAN + Exposure. Brokers cannot allow positions where collected margin falls below this total.

VAR Margin (Equity Cash)

Value at Risk margin for equity delivery positions. Based on statistical daily price range (99% confidence interval, 1-day holding period). Calculated by NSE/BSE using historical price data.

Liquid large-cap stocks: VAR typically 5 to 8%. Illiquid small-caps: VAR can be 30%+. Updated daily by exchange and published on NSE website.

ELM (Extreme Loss Margin)

Extreme Loss Margin for equity. Additional buffer over VAR for tail-risk scenarios not captured by statistical models. Exchange prescribed, currently 1.5% to 5% depending on stock liquidity category.

Total Equity Intraday Margin = VAR + ELM. For delivery buy positions, SEBI's new rule requires upfront collection of VAR + ELM on buy side as well.

Peak Margin Requirement (Post Sep 2021)
📋

SEBI Circular: Upfront Margin Collection

SEBI circular SEBI/HO/MRD2/DCAP/CIR/P/2020/127 (Jul 2020) introduced peak margin reporting. Implemented in phases. From September 2021, brokers must collect 100% of the required margin upfront before allowing any new position. The exchange takes four intraday snapshots to check peak margin. Shortfall triggers a penalty: 0.5% per day for first 3 days, 1% per day thereafter.

This regulation eliminated the practice of leveraging clients beyond their capital. Before peak margin, brokers routinely offered 10x to 20x intraday leverage. Post Sep 2021, leverage is constrained to the margin framework.

Mark to Market (MTM) Settlement

Daily MTM for F&O Positions

All open futures positions are settled daily to their closing price. The daily settlement price for index futures is the weighted average of the last 30 minutes of trading. For stock futures, it is the last traded price subject to a ±1% circuit filter.

Worked Example: You are long 1 lot of Nifty Futures bought at 22,000. Closing price on Day 1: 21,800. MTM loss = (22,000 - 21,800) x 75 = Rs.15,000. This Rs.15,000 is debited from your account overnight. If account balance falls below minimum margin, you receive a margin call. Failure to top up by next morning leads to auto-square off.

Margin Pledge and Re-Pledge System
📋

New Pledge System (SEBI 2020)

Prior to August 2020, brokers held client securities in a pool account to offer as collateral. SEBI circular SEBI/HO/MIRSD/MIRSD2/CIR/P/2020/28 mandated a pledge/re-pledge mechanism. Clients must now explicitly pledge securities to the broker via the depository system (NSDL/CDSL), and brokers re-pledge to clearing corporations. Securities never leave the client's demat account, eliminating broker misuse risk. Haircut: 15 to 50% depending on security.

RMS (Risk Management System)

What RMS Does

  • Validates margin availability before every order
  • Sets maximum loss limits per client per day
  • Triggers auto-square off if MTM loss exceeds configured threshold
  • Blocks accounts with debit balances or insufficient margin
  • Generates margin calls and payment reminders
  • Reports peak margin snapshots to exchange

RMS Parameters Set by Broker

  • Max loss limit: Auto-square-off if daily loss exceeds X%
  • Scrip-level limits: Max position in illiquid or circuit-bound stocks
  • Segment limits: Max exposure in F&O vs equity
  • Net worth limit: Cap on total exposure relative to client's declared income
  • Freeze list: Stocks blocked for trading (surveillance, circuit, operator activity)
Compliance & KYC

Compliance is not a back-office function. It is a first-line-of-defense discipline. Regulatory violations in broking attract heavy penalties, debarment, and reputational damage. Know the rules.

KYC Framework

Documents Required (Individual Client)

  • PAN Card: Mandatory for all investment accounts. Linked to Aadhaar since 2017. SEBI rule: no PAN = no trading account
  • Aadhaar: Used for eKYC and address proof. Masked Aadhaar accepted for privacy
  • Bank Account Proof: Cancelled cheque or bank statement (must match account holder name)
  • Photograph: Recent passport size or captured digitally
  • Income Proof: For F&O trading: 6 months bank statement, ITR, or salary slip. Required to assess financial suitability
  • Signature: Physical or digital

KYC Process Flow

  1. Client submits documents (physical or digital via app/DigiLocker)
  2. In-Person Verification (IPV): Client must be verified physically or via video KYC
  3. Data uploaded to KRA (KYC Registration Agency): CAMS KRA, Karvy KRA, NDML, CDSL Ventures
  4. CKYC (Central KYC Registry under CERSAI): One-time KYC for all financial services
  5. Account activation after exchange registration and UCC (Unique Client Code) generation
  6. Risk profiling for F&O segment access
📋

Nomination Mandate (SEBI 2023)

SEBI circular dated July 2022 (effective March 2023) made nomination mandatory for all demat and trading accounts. Existing clients had to either add a nominee or submit a declaration opting out. Non-compliance resulted in account freeze for debits. As of 2023, brokers had to ensure 100% compliance for active accounts.

PMLA Obligations

Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) 2002

Brokers are "Reporting Entities" under PMLA. This means specific obligations:

  • CTR (Cash Transaction Report): Report all cash transactions above Rs.10 lakh to FIU-IND (Financial Intelligence Unit) within 7 days
  • STR (Suspicious Transaction Report): Report any transaction that appears unusual or suspicious regardless of amount, within 7 days of suspicion
  • Record Keeping: Maintain all client records for minimum 5 years
  • Client Due Diligence (CDD): Enhanced due diligence for politically exposed persons (PEPs), high-value clients, and clients from high-risk jurisdictions
  • Principal Officer: Designated compliance officer responsible for PMLA reporting to FIU-IND

DDPI: Replacing the POA
💡

DDPI (Demat Debit and Pledge Instruction)

Prior to 2022, brokers collected a blanket Power of Attorney (POA) from clients authorizing the broker to debit the demat account. SEBI circular (October 2022) replaced this with DDPI, effective April 1, 2023. DDPI limits the broker's authority to only three actions: (1) settlement of trades, (2) pledging for margin, and (3) tender of securities in open offers/buybacks. The broker cannot misuse DDPI to move shares for non-trading purposes.

Revenue Model

Understanding how a brokerage makes money tells you what gets prioritized, what products get pushed, and where the incentives lie. Know the P&L before you join the desk.

Revenue Streams
📈

Brokerage Income

Core revenue. Fee charged per transaction. Two dominant models:

Full-Service: Percentage of trade value. Typically 0.2 to 0.5% on equity delivery, 0.02 to 0.05% on F&O turnover. High-value HNI accounts negotiated lower.

Discount: Flat Rs.20 per executed order (Zerodha/Upstox model) or zero for equity delivery. Revenue driven by high order volume and F&O activity.

🏦

Interest Income / Margin Funding

Brokers with NBFC (Non-Banking Financial Company) arms offer margin trading facility (MTF) to clients. Clients borrow to buy stocks, broker charges 18 to 24% annualized interest on the funded amount.

Also: float income on client funds maintained in the broker's bank accounts. Large brokers hold thousands of crores in client credit balances overnight, earning overnight call money rates.

📦

DP (Depository Participant) Charges

Brokers act as DPs for NSDL or CDSL. Revenue from:

  • Account opening fee (one-time, increasingly waived)
  • Annual maintenance charge (Rs.200 to Rs.750 per year)
  • Transaction charges: typically Rs.13 to Rs.20 per debit instruction (demat debit on sale)
  • Pledge creation and closure charges

DP income is small per transaction but scales with millions of accounts.

💼

Distribution Income

Commissions earned by distributing third-party products:

  • Mutual Funds: Trail commission (0.4 to 1% p.a. on AUM under distribution). Requires ARN. No upfront commission since 2018 SEBI circular.
  • Insurance: First-year and renewal commissions. Requires IRDA license via partner or own subsidiary
  • IPOs: ASBA facilitation fee, anchor book building involvement for large brokers
  • Bonds/NCD: Distribution fee from issuer
📊

Research and Advisory

Full-service brokers monetize research through:

  • Paid research subscriptions (Rs.5,000 to Rs.50,000 per year for retail)
  • Institutional research: sold to FPIs, AIFs, domestic MFs. Revenue via soft dollar arrangements or direct fees
  • Investment Advisory (IA): SEBI-registered IA can charge clients for financial planning. Capped at 2.5% of AUM per year or Rs.75,000 flat per annum (SEBI IA regulations 2020)
🔧

Other Income

  • Call and Trade: Rs.20 to Rs.50 per order placed via phone (offline surcharge)
  • Algo / API Access: Subscription fees for automated trading API access
  • Account Transfer Charges: Fee for transferring positions to/from other brokers
  • SMS/2FA premium: Some brokers charge for additional OTP services
  • Corporate Actions: Custody fees on dividends, rights issues, buyback tendering

The F&O Risk for Discount Brokers

Discount brokers generate disproportionate revenue from F&O trading. SEBI data shows over 90% of F&O traders lose money. Regulatory changes targeting F&O volumes (lot size increases, strikes rationalization) directly threaten this revenue stream. SEBI's consultation paper on F&O measures (2024) has significant implications for discount broker economics.

Career & Certifications

The broking industry offers varied career paths: client-facing, research, risk, operations, technology, and compliance. Here is the map.

Career Tracks

Sales and Relationship Management

Roles: AP, Dealer, Relationship Manager (RM), Branch Manager, Key Account Manager (KAM for HNIs and corporates).

Day-to-day: Client acquisition, portfolio reviews, product recommendations, handling client queries, margin reminders, resolving disputes.

Compensation: Base + variable (typically 20 to 40% of gross brokerage generated from portfolio). High performers earn Rs.8 to Rs.25 lakh CTC in first 3 years.

Equity Research

Roles: Research Associate, Equity Analyst, Senior Analyst, Head of Research.

Day-to-day: Financial modelling, earnings analysis, sector reports, company visits, quarterly result notes, thematic research.

SEBI Requirement: Research Analyst (RA) registration with SEBI required to publish research. Exam: NISM Series XV (Research Analyst).

Salary range: Rs.5 to Rs.15 lakh (associate level). Senior analyst at top brokers: Rs.30 to Rs.80 lakh.

Dealing and Trading

Roles: Dealer, Senior Dealer, Head Dealer. Institutional desks have Equity Sales Trader, Execution Trader.

Day-to-day: Executing client orders on the terminal, monitoring positions, managing order books, handling Call and Trade orders.

Exam: NCFM Dealers Module or NISM Series VII (Securities Operations). Dealer terminal access requires exchange-approved certification.

Risk Management

Roles: Risk Analyst, RMS Executive, Chief Risk Officer.

Day-to-day: Monitoring real-time margin utilization, managing auto-square-off parameters, setting client limits, margin shortfall tracking, peak margin reporting.

Risk roles are increasingly critical post-peak-margin regulations. Strong understanding of SPAN, VAR, and options Greeks essential.

Compliance

Roles: Compliance Executive, Principal Officer (PMLA), Chief Compliance Officer (CCO).

Day-to-day: SEBI/Exchange regulatory filings, internal policy updates, audit coordination, KYC oversight, investor grievance management via SEBI SCORES, surveillance reports.

CCO must be a Key Managerial Personnel. Qualification requirement: CS (Company Secretary) or Law graduate preferred. NISM Series VII mandatory.

Operations (Back Office)

Roles: Settlements, DP Operations, Fund Accounting, Reconciliation, IT/Tech.

Day-to-day: Reconciling exchange pay-in/pay-out, DP settlement, contract note generation, client ledger maintenance, fund transfer processing.

Often the entry point for finance graduates. Strong Excel, back-office software (Flattrade, Omnesys, OMEX) skills valued.

NISM Certifications (Mandatory and Recommended)
NISM SERIES I
Currency Derivatives
Mandatory for currency segment dealers and APs handling currency products. 100 questions, 2 hours, passing 60%.
NISM SERIES V-A
Mutual Fund Distributors
Required for ARN (AMFI Registration Number). Mandatory to distribute MFs. Most common entry-level certification.
NISM SERIES VII
Securities Operations and Risk Management
Recommended for operations, compliance, and dealing roles. Covers clearing, settlement, regulatory framework.
NISM SERIES VIII
Equity Derivatives
Required for equity F&O dealers and APs. Covers futures, options, Greeks, clearing. Very important for F&O desk roles.
NISM SERIES X-A & X-B
Investment Adviser (Level 1 and 2)
Required for SEBI Investment Adviser (IA) registration. Level 1: products and markets. Level 2: financial planning and portfolio management.
NISM SERIES XV
Research Analyst
Required for SEBI Research Analyst registration. For equity research roles at brokers. Covers fundamental analysis, research report standards.
NCFM DEALERS MODULE
NSE's Dealer Certification
NSE's own certification for dealer terminal access. Required for sub-broker and AP roles under NSE guidelines. Separate from NISM.
CFA (CFA INSTITUTE)
Chartered Financial Analyst
International gold standard for investment research. 3 levels. Highly valued for research analyst and portfolio management roles. Not mandatory but career-defining.
💡

Exam Strategy

For a fresh finance graduate, certify in this order: (1) NISM Series V-A for MF distribution license, (2) NISM Series VIII for F&O desk credibility, (3) NISM Series VII for operations and compliance understanding. Combined cost under Rs.5,000. Returns immediate employability in front, middle, and back office roles.
